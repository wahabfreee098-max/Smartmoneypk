How to build an emergency fund in Pakistan (even on a small salary)

A step-by-step plan to build a financial safety net, starting with whatever you can afford.

📅 June 2026⏱ 7 min read✍️ SmartMoney PK Team

An emergency fund is money set aside purely for unexpected expenses — a medical bill, sudden job loss, urgent home repair, or family emergency. Without one, a single bad month can push a family into debt. Building one in Pakistan is absolutely possible, even on a modest salary.

Why you need this before anything else

Before investing in stocks, gold, or property, financial experts agree: build your emergency fund first. It is the foundation everything else stands on. Without it, a single emergency can force you to sell investments at a loss or take an expensive loan.

How much should you save?

The standard recommendation is 3-6 months of essential expenses (rent, food, utilities, transport — not luxuries). For a household spending Rs 50,000/month on essentials, that means a target of Rs 150,000-300,000.

💡 Start smaller if needed

If that number feels overwhelming, start with a goal of just Rs 20,000. Having something is far better than having nothing.

Step 1: Open a separate account

Don't keep emergency savings in the same account you use daily — you'll be tempted to dip into it. Open a separate savings account, ideally one that earns profit, such as Meezan Bank's Bachat account or a similar Islamic savings product from any major bank.

Step 2: Automate the transfer

The moment your salary lands, transfer a fixed amount — even Rs 2,000 — to your emergency account before you spend on anything else. This "pay yourself first" approach works because it removes willpower from the equation.

Step 3: Use windfalls wisely

Eid bonuses, tax refunds, or any unexpected money should go straight into this fund until you hit your target. It's tempting to spend windfalls, but they're the fastest way to build your safety net.

Step 4: Only touch it for real emergencies

Define in advance what counts: medical emergencies, job loss, essential home or vehicle repairs. A sale at your favourite store does not qualify. Writing this rule down before an emergency happens makes it much easier to follow under stress.

What NOT to do

  • Don't invest your emergency fund in stocks or property — it needs to be accessible within a day or two.
  • Don't keep it as cash at home — bank profit, even modest, beats zero return and theft risk.
  • Don't stop contributing once you hit your goal — let it grow slightly with inflation each year.

A realistic timeline

Saving Rs 5,000/month, a household can build a Rs 60,000 emergency fund in one year. That's enough to cover most unexpected expenses without taking on debt or selling other investments.
