Is buying property still worth it in Pakistan in 2026?

We break down the real numbers — rental yields, capital gains, and risks.

📅 June 2026⏱ 7 min read✍️ SmartMoney PK Team

Real estate has long been considered Pakistan's favourite investment — but rising prices, documentation requirements, and changing tax rules mean the calculation isn't as simple as it used to be. Here's a realistic look at the numbers.

Rental yields are often lower than people assume

Many investors focus on capital appreciation (the property's price going up) and overlook rental yield (annual rent as a percentage of property value). In most major Pakistani cities, rental yields on residential property typically range from 2-5% annually — often lower than what you'd earn from a bank savings certificate with far less hassle.

Where property has historically performed well

Areas near new infrastructure projects — ring roads, metro lines, new commercial zones — have historically seen stronger appreciation than already-developed areas. But these are also higher-risk, since infrastructure projects can be delayed for years.

💡 Do your homework

Verify land titles through the relevant development authority before any purchase. Property fraud involving fake or disputed titles is unfortunately common in Pakistan.

The hidden costs of property investment

  • Stamp duty and registration fees: Often 3-7% of property value, depending on province.
  • Capital gains tax: Applies if you sell within a certain holding period — check current FBR rules as they change periodically.
  • Maintenance: Ongoing costs that eat into rental income.
  • Illiquidity: Unlike stocks, you can't sell a portion of a property — selling can take months.

Property vs other investments — a fair comparison

Property requires a large upfront amount (often millions of rupees), is illiquid, and has high transaction costs. Mutual funds and stocks allow you to start with much smaller amounts and sell quickly if needed. Neither is universally "better" — it depends on your goals, timeline, and how much capital you have.

Questions to ask before buying

  • Can I verify this property's title is clean and undisputed?
  • What's the realistic rental yield, not just the agent's promise of appreciation?
  • Do I have enough liquid savings left after this purchase for emergencies?
  • Am I buying because I genuinely believe in the location, or because everyone around me is buying property?

The bottom line

Property can still be a reasonable part of a long-term Pakistani investment portfolio, especially for those with significant capital and a long time horizon. But it shouldn't be your only investment, and the decision should be based on verified numbers — not assumptions inherited from previous decades when the market behaved differently.
