The 50/30/20 budgeting rule, adapted for Pakistani salaries

A simple framework to organize your monthly income without complicated spreadsheets.

📅 June 2026⏱ 5 min read✍️ SmartMoney PK Team

The 50/30/20 rule is one of the simplest budgeting frameworks in personal finance: 50% of income on needs, 30% on wants, and 20% on savings and debt repayment. Here's how to apply it realistically to a Pakistani salary.

The 50% — needs

This covers rent or mortgage, groceries, utilities (electricity, gas, water), transport to work, and essential phone/internet. For many Pakistani households, especially in expensive cities like Karachi or Lahore, rent alone can consume a large portion of this category — if so, it's worth reviewing whether your housing situation fits your income realistically.

The 30% — wants

Eating out, entertainment, subscriptions, shopping beyond necessities, and similar discretionary spending. This category is where most "invisible" overspending happens — small purchases that don't feel significant individually but add up by month's end.

💡 Track for one month before judging yourself

Before changing anything, simply track where your money currently goes for 30 days. Most people are surprised by how their actual spending differs from what they assumed.

The 20% — savings and debt repayment

This includes building your emergency fund, contributing to investments, and paying down any debt beyond minimum payments. If you currently have high-interest debt (credit cards, personal loans), prioritise paying that down before aggressive investing — the interest saved often exceeds typical investment returns.

Adjusting the ratios for reality

The 50/30/20 split is a starting guideline, not a rigid law. If your rent and essential costs genuinely exceed 50% of your income — common in major Pakistani cities — you may need to start with something like 65/20/15 and gradually shift the balance as your income grows or you find ways to reduce essential costs.

Making it work in practice

  • Set up automatic transfers for your savings percentage on payday, before you have a chance to spend it.
  • Use a simple notes app or basic spreadsheet — you don't need complicated software to track three categories.
  • Review and adjust monthly rather than expecting to get it perfect immediately.

Why this simple structure works

Most people don't fail at budgeting because of bad math — they fail because complicated systems are hard to maintain. A three-category framework is simple enough to stick with for months and years, which matters far more than a perfectly optimised but abandoned system.
